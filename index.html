Auction Strategy FAQ

In the team table, each round shows two percentages:

  • First percentage: This is the team's probability of reaching that round, calculated from devigged odds. For example, if a team has a 75% chance to make the Round of 32, this number would be 75.00%.
  • Second percentage (in parentheses): This is the team's value percentage for that round, calculated by multiplying their probability by the round's payout percentage. For example, if a team has a 75% chance to make the Round of 32 and the Round of 32 pays 0.5% of the pot, this number would be 0.375% (75% × 0.5%).

These percentages help you understand both the likelihood of success and the potential value at each stage of the tournament.

Fair Value is what a team is mathematically worth based on their probability of winning. It's calculated by multiplying the team's value percentage by the total pot size.

Suggested Bid is set at 95% of the Fair Value. This creates a 5% buffer that gives you potential profit if you win the team at that price.

This strategy (known as "bid shading" in auction theory) ensures you don't overpay for teams and can build a profitable portfolio.

The Suggested Bid is your target maximum bid for each team. If you can win a team at or below this price, you're getting good value.

In practice:

  • Start bidding below the Suggested Bid
  • Be willing to go up to the Suggested Bid if necessary
  • Avoid exceeding the Suggested Bid unless you have strategic reasons
  • Remember that winning teams below their Suggested Bid increases your overall profit potential

If you consistently bid exactly at Fair Value:

  • You would theoretically break even in the long run (not accounting for variance)
  • You leave no room for profit margin
  • Any slight overestimation of a team's chances would result in a loss

By bidding below Fair Value, you build in a profit margin that helps ensure positive expected value across your portfolio of teams.

The profit projections in the table show two different types of information depending on whether a team has been purchased:

  • For teams without a purchase price: The values show the raw payout amount that team would earn for reaching each round. This helps you understand the potential earnings before making a bid.
  • For teams with a purchase price: The values show your actual profit (payout minus purchase price) if the team reaches that round. This helps you track your potential returns on purchased teams.

Below each profit value, you'll see two percentages:

  • First percentage: The team's probability of reaching that round (based on devigged odds)
  • Second percentage (in parentheses): The team's value percentage for that round (probability × payout percentage)

These percentages help you assess both the likelihood of success and the potential value at each stage of the tournament.

Timing Your Bids
  • Bid early: Teams often go for less at the beginning when participants are still feeling out the process. As the auction progresses, people realize they don't have enough teams and may overpay.
  • Exception: If your Calcutta has a spending limit, consider waiting until the end to snatch up value when others are approaching their limits.
First Team Sets the Market
  • The winning bid for the first team sets the relative value for every other team auctioned after it.
  • This establishes the projected pot size - if a team worth 2% of the prize pool goes for $100, participants will estimate a $5,000 total pot.
Strategic Bidding
  • Drive up the pot: Consider bidding up teams you don't necessarily want, especially when there's no spending limit. A higher pot benefits everyone who already owns teams.
  • Be careful: There's always a risk the other bidder will stop and you'll be stuck with your bid.
  • Target fan favorites: If you notice someone is a big fan of a particular team, they may be willing to overpay. This can be an opportunity to drive up the pot.
Portfolio Management
  • Avoid bracket conflicts: Be strategic about buying teams in the same region. If you have the #1 and #2 seed in the same region, one team is capped at three wins.
  • Regional strategy: Consider "cornering" a region by fading an overvalued top seed and acquiring other strong teams in that bracket.
  • Diversify: Take positions in different regions to minimize the chances of your teams eliminating each other.
Late Auction Opportunities
  • Exploit FOMO: Participants who haven't won any teams yet may overpay to avoid missing out completely.
  • Auction fatigue: When everyone already has teams, bidding action often decreases, creating value for those still willing to bid.
